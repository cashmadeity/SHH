# Crisis Communication Scripts & Protocols

**Purpose:** Provide word-for-word scripts and decision frameworks for recognizing and responding to mental health crises in therapeutic communication contexts.

**Foundation:** Crisis intervention training (CIT), suicide prevention protocols (QPR, safeTALK), trauma-informed care, and emergency medical standards.

**Target audience:** Mental health app designers, chatbot creators, therapists using technology, crisis hotline operators, educators.

---

## Core Principle: Speed + Clarity + Connection

In crisis:
1. **Recognize** it's a crisis (not optional escalation)
2. **Respond** clearly (no ambiguity or delay)
3. **Stay present** while escalating (don't abandon them)
4. **Connect to help** (not just refer, but actively connect)

---

## 1. Crisis Recognition: What Requires Immediate Escalation

### Red Flags for Acute Danger

**Suicidality:**
- "I want to hurt myself"
- "Everyone would be better off without me"
- "I can't take this anymore"
- Plans/intent mentioned (even vague)
- Giving away possessions
- Final goodbyes

**Homicidality/Violence:**
- "I'm going to hurt them"
- Detailed plans to harm someone
- Access to weapons mentioned
- Recent history of violence

**Psychosis/Severe Dissociation:**
- "The government is controlling my thoughts"
- "I'm not real / I don't exist"
- "I can hear voices telling me to..."
- Inability to distinguish reality

**Severe Intoxication/Overdose:**
- Unclear thinking, incoherence
- "I took a bunch of pills"
- Difficulty breathing, slurred speech
- Unresponsiveness

**Acute Trauma Activation:**
- Flashback with loss of time sense
- "I can't tell what's real"
- Extreme panic (panic attack lasting 30+ minutes)
- Self-harm urge in response to trauma trigger

**Abuse/Violence Happening Now:**
- "They're hitting me right now"
- "I'm trapped and can't get out"
- Active threat in immediate environment

### Moderate/Elevated Concerns (Still Requires Escalation)

- Recent suicide attempt (within 48 hours)
- Current plan to self-harm (cutting, burning, etc.)
- Acute depression with complete hopelessness (feels different from baseline)
- Recent significant loss/trauma with destabilization
- Substance abuse escalation with new symptoms
- Inability to care for self (not eating, hygiene, safety)

## 2. Crisis Recognition Script & Decision Tree

### Initial Assessment

```
PERSON: (mentions something concerning)

YOU: PAUSE AND ASSESS

Ask clearly:

"I want to make sure you're safe.
Are you thinking about hurting yourself right now?"

(Direct, not evasive. Use the word "hurt" or "kill"—don't avoid it.)

THEY SAY "YES" → Go to SUICIDALITY SCRIPT (3.1)
THEY SAY "NO" but expressed concerning statement → Ask specificity
THEY AVOID ANSWERING → "I need to know you're safe. Are you thinking about it?"
```

### Decision Tree: What Escalation Level?

```
┌─ Is person in immediate danger to self/others?
│  ├─ YES → IMMEDIATE CRISIS (call 911, activate emergency)
│  └─ NO → continue
│
├─ Does person have clear plan/intent/recent attempt?
│  ├─ YES → HIGH RISK (crisis line + immediate human support)
│  └─ NO → continue
│
├─ Is person expressing hopelessness + suicidal ideation?
│  ├─ YES → MODERATE RISK (crisis line + follow-up within 24h)
│  └─ NO → continue
│
└─ Is this an ethical/professional concern requiring escalation?
   ├─ YES → Contact person's therapist/emergency contact
   └─ NO → Standard therapeutic response + safety planning
```

## 3. Suicidality Scripts

### 3.1 When Someone Expresses Suicidal Thoughts

```
PERSON: "I don't think I can do this anymore. 
I'm thinking about ending it."

YOUR RESPONSE (in order):

STEP 1: VALIDATE & TAKE SERIOUSLY
"I'm really glad you told me that. 
Suicidal thoughts are serious, and I'm taking them seriously right now."

(NOT: "Don't say that" / "You shouldn't feel that way")

STEP 2: ASSESS SAFETY
"Tell me more. Are you thinking about it, or do you have a plan?"

→ If YES to plan: Move to STEP 4 (ESCALATE)
→ If NO to plan: Continue to STEP 3

STEP 3: EXPLORE PROTECTIVE FACTORS
"What's kept you going so far? 
What or who matters enough to stay?"

(Listen for: relationships, values, unfinished goals, responsibility)

"Have you ever felt this way before? What helped then?"

STEP 4: ESCALATE IMMEDIATELY
"I need to connect you with people trained for this.
This is beyond what I can handle alone.

I'm connecting you with the 988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline right now.
They're real people, available 24/7, and they know how to help.

Let me get you the number:
988 (call) or text "HELLO" to 741741 (Crisis Text Line)

Are you in a safe place right now to reach out to them?"

STEP 5: DON'T LEAVE THEM HANGING
"I'm staying here while you contact them.
Or if you prefer, we can call together.

What feels safest for you?"

(Give them choice of: calling now, texting now, you calling, etc.)

STEP 6: SAFETY PLAN
Once connected to crisis support, create a written safety plan:
- Warning signs that suicidality is rising
- Internal coping strategies
- People/places that provide distraction
- People to tell ("If I'm thinking this, I'll call...")
- Professional resources (therapist, psychiatrist, hospital)
- Reasons for living (written, they review when in crisis)

STEP 7: FOLLOW-UP
(Human therapist or professional follow-up within 24 hours minimum)

After crisis call/text:
"When this conversation is done, I want to check in with you tomorrow.
What time works best?"

(Shows continuity of care, not abandonment)
```

### 3.2 When Someone Has a Plan

```
PERSON: "I've been thinking about taking pills. 
I have a whole bottle of my antidepressants sitting here."

YOUR RESPONSE:

IMMEDIATE: TREAT AS IMMINENT DANGER

"You have access to means and a plan. 
This is an emergency.

I'm going to help you get to people right now who can keep you safe.

Are you in the US?"

IF YES: 
"I'm calling 988 for you right now. 
Or, if you're in immediate danger, we're calling 911.

If you're alone, can you move to where someone is?
Or should I call emergency services?"

IF NO (different country):
"I'm getting you to your local crisis service:
[COUNTRY-SPECIFIC NUMBER]

If you're in danger right now, we're calling emergency services.
Your safety is the priority."

CRITICAL ACTIONS:
- [ ] Don't minimize ("You won't actually do it")
- [ ] Don't shame ("How could you think that?")
- [ ] Don't blame yourself ("I should have seen this")
- [ ] DO: Act quickly and directly
- [ ] DO: Involve others (roommate, family, emergency services)
- [ ] DO: Remove access to means if possible
- [ ] DO: Stay with them until help arrives (physically or virtually)
```

### 3.3 When Someone Dismisses Suicidal Thoughts

```
PERSON: "I've been thinking about it, but it's nothing. I'll be fine."

❌ DON'T ACCEPT AT FACE VALUE:
(Minimize = enable)

✅ DO: TAKE SERIOUSLY
"Suicidal thoughts are never 'nothing.'
Even if you think you won't act on them, 
the fact that you're having them means you're suffering.

Tell me: If things don't get better, do you think you might act on these thoughts?"

(Test for what therapists call "passive vs. active" suicidality)

PASSIVE: "I think about it but wouldn't do it"
→ Still concerning; needs support and safety plan

ACTIVE: "I might actually do it"
→ Immediate escalation to crisis services
```

### 3.4 When Someone Won't Admit Suicidality But You're Concerned

```
PERSON: "I'm fine. Don't worry."
(But they're expressing hopelessness, talking about being a burden, etc.)

YOU: TRUST YOUR CLINICAL INSTINCT

"I hear you saying you're fine.
And I'm also noticing something else.
You've mentioned feeling like a burden and not having energy for anything.

That combination concerns me.
When people feel that way, sometimes they think about not existing.
Is that happening for you?"

(Name the pattern → open door for disclosure)

If still resistant:
"Sometimes people minimize suicidal thoughts because 
they're embarrassed or don't think anyone will understand.
I want you to know: you can tell me. It's safe to say it."
```

## 4. Self-Harm Scripts (Non-Suicidal)

### When Someone Expresses Self-Harm Urges

```
PERSON: "I've been cutting myself.
I know it's not healthy, but it's the only thing that helps."

YOUR RESPONSE:

STEP 1: VALIDATE THE FUNCTION
"Cutting helps you manage overwhelming feelings right now.
That's important information.
It means you're trying to regulate something intense."

(NOT: "That's bad. Stop doing that.")

STEP 2: UNDERSTAND THE PATTERN
"What feeling are you trying to manage?
What happens right before you feel the urge?
What does it feel like in your body?"

(Build understanding of the cycle)

STEP 3: ASSESS SAFETY
"Are you in immediate danger of seriously injuring yourself?
Have you thought about wanting to die?"

→ If YES (suicidal): Escalate to SUICIDALITY PROTOCOL
→ If NO (self-harm only): Continue to STEP 4

STEP 4: BUILD ALTERNATIVES
"We're not going to shame you for coping this way.
Your nervous system needs something intense to feel regulated.

Let's find other intense sensations that might help:
- Holding ice in your hands
- Intense exercise
- Cold shower
- Snapping rubber band on wrist
- Loud music
- Intense breathing (breathing out longer than in)

What intensity level do you need?
What sounds remotely possible to try?"

STEP 5: PROFESSIONAL SUPPORT
"Self-harm is often a sign that something deeper needs support.
Have you worked with a therapist on this?

If not, let's get you connected to someone who specializes in this.
[Provide resources]

If yes: Tell your therapist about increased urges. This might mean
your treatment plan needs adjustment."

STEP 6: SAFETY PLAN FOR SELF-HARM
- Identify warning signs (feeling, time of day, situation)
- List alternatives (ranked by intensity)
- Identify people to contact
- Plan for if alternatives fail ("Then I'll call this person")
- Commit to professional support

STEP 7: FOLLOW-UP
"I want to check in with you in [specific timeframe].
How are the alternatives working?"

(Ongoing support, not one-time conversation)
```

## 5. Homicidality & Violence Risk Scripts

### When Someone Expresses Intent to Harm Others

```
PERSON: "I'm so angry at my boss. 
I fantasize about hurting them."

ASSESS SERIOUSNESS:

Ask directly (not evasive):
"Are you thinking about actually hurting them,
or is this fantasy to cope with anger?"

THEY SAY "JUST FANTASY":
"What does the fantasy do for you?
How does it help?"

(Listen. Fantasies often help people regulate.)

"Have you ever acted on violence before?"

IF NO + LOW RISK FACTORS:
"Anger is understandable. Fantasizing happens.
Let's work on what's actually happening with your boss.
What would help you feel less powerless?"

→ Standard therapy response, not emergency escalation

---

THEY SAY "I MIGHT ACTUALLY DO IT" OR EXPRESS DETAILED PLAN:

IMMEDIATE ESCALATION:

"That's a serious threat. I need to take this seriously.

Tell me:
- Do you have access to weapons?
- Have you thought about when/where?
- Do you have the ability to do this?
- What's stopping you right now?"

(Assess: plan, access, intent, timeframe)

IF ANY "YES" + IMMINENT THREAT:

"This is a safety issue. I'm required by law to:
1) Contact emergency services, and/or
2) Notify the person at risk, and/or
3) Notify your therapist/psychiatrist

I'm doing this because I take your safety and theirs seriously.

Right now, I need to get you to professional help."

ACTIONS:
- [ ] Call 911 if imminent danger
- [ ] Notify threat target (legal requirement in most places)
- [ ] Notify treating therapist/psychiatrist
- [ ] Document everything
- [ ] Don't promise confidentiality ("I can't keep this secret")

LANGUAGE:
"I care about your wellbeing AND about the safety of others.
That means I sometimes have to break confidentiality.
This is one of those times."
```

## 6. Psychosis & Acute Confusion Scripts

### When Someone Expresses Psychotic Symptoms

```
PERSON: "The government is monitoring my thoughts.
They've put a chip in my brain.
I need to protect myself."

DO NOT:
- Agree with the delusion ("Yes, they are!")
- Aggressively challenge ("That's not real, you're crazy")
- Gaslight ("You know that's not true")
- Panic or treat them as dangerous

DO:

STEP 1: ASSESS SAFETY
"Are you thinking about hurting yourself or others because of this?"

→ If YES: Escalate (they may act on delusion)
→ If NO: Continue

STEP 2: STAY GROUNDED & PRESENT
"I believe you're experiencing something that feels very real.
And I also know this sounds like it could be a psychiatric symptom
that medication can help with.

Have you talked to a psychiatrist about this?"

(Validate experience without validating delusion)

STEP 3: ENCOURAGE PROFESSIONAL SUPPORT
"These kinds of experiences—thoughts you can't control,
feeling like something's been done to your body—
are sometimes signs that your brain chemistry needs support.

A psychiatrist can help with that.
Have you worked with one before?"

IF YES: "Can we reach out to them right now?"
IF NO: "I want to help you get to one."

STEP 4: IF THEY REFUSE HELP
"I'm concerned about your safety and wellbeing.
If you're not willing to see a psychiatrist,
I need to contact [family member / therapist / emergency services]
to make sure you get support."

(Don't force, but escalate)

STEP 5: HOSPITALIZATION CONSIDERATION
Recommend hospitalization if:
- Psychosis is worsening
- Danger to self/others
- Unable to care for basic needs
- No support system at home

"The hospital isn't a punishment.
It's a place where psychiatrists can help stabilize your brain chemistry.
Some people find real relief there."
```

## 7. Acute Grief & Trauma Scripts

### When Someone Experiences Acute Grief (Loss)

```
PERSON: (just lost someone)
"I can't believe they're gone. 
This doesn't feel real.
I don't know how to live without them."

YOUR RESPONSE:

VALIDATE THE SHOCK:
"The shock of losing them is immense.
Your mind is protecting you right now—
it hasn't fully landed because it's too much."

VALIDATE THE PAIN:
"This is the hardest kind of pain.
There's no 'right way' to grieve.
Some days will feel impossible."

ASSESS DANGER:
"Are you having thoughts about wanting to die?
Or about harming yourself?"

→ If YES: Suicidality protocol
→ If NO: Continue

OFFER PRESENCE:
"I'm here. We can sit with this.
Tell me about them."

(Let them talk. Grief heals through telling the story.)

PROVIDE STRUCTURE:
"Right now, the days are going to feel chaotic.
Let's make sure you have:
- Someone checking on you daily
- Basic needs covered (eating, sleeping)
- Grief support (group or therapist)
- Their funeral/service planned (if applicable)

What do you need most right now?"

CREATE SAFETY PLAN:
"When grief gets overwhelming, you'll:
1) Contact [person]
2) Go to [place]
3) Do [grounding activity]
4) Call [number]"

NORMALIZE THE TIMELINE:
"Grief doesn't have a deadline.
You might feel okay tomorrow and devastated next week.
That's normal.

Professional grief counseling can help."
```

### When Someone Has Trauma Flashback

```
PERSON: (activated, experiencing flashback)
"I can't... it's happening again... 
I can't breathe... get away from me..."

YOUR RESPONSE:

THEY ARE NOT IN THE PRESENT MOMENT.

STEP 1: GENTLE GROUNDING
(Soft, calm voice—don't startle them)

"You're safe. You're here now.
The scary thing happened before.
Right now, you're with me. You're safe."

STEP 2: GROUND IN SENSES
"Look around. What do you see?
Tell me 5 things you can see right now."

(Engages present-moment senses)

"What do you feel under your hands?
The chair/floor/blanket? Feel it. You're here."

(Grounding through touch)

STEP 3: BREATHING
"Let's slow your breathing.
Breathe in for 4... hold for 4... out for 6...

You're bringing oxygen to your brain.
You're safe."

STEP 4: DON'T TOUCH WITHOUT ASKING
"Can I sit with you?"
"Can I hold your hand?"

(Trauma survivors can perceive touch as threat)

STEP 5: STAY WITH THEM
"I'm right here. You're not alone."

(Presence is the medicine)

STEP 6: AFTER GROUNDING
"You had a flashback. Your brain was reliving something scary.
It felt real because your nervous system was activated.
But you're safe now.

What helped bring you back?"

STEP 7: PROFESSIONAL SUPPORT
"Flashbacks are a sign your nervous system needs trauma treatment.
Have you worked with a trauma specialist?

Therapies like EMDR or trauma-focused CBT can help rewire those reactions."

ESCALATION:
If they're self-harming during flashback or expressing suicide:
Follow SELF-HARM or SUICIDALITY protocol
```

## 8. Substance-Related Crisis Scripts

### When Someone Is Intoxicated or Overdosing

```
PERSON: (incoherent, slurred speech)
"I took some pills... don't know how many...
I'm feeling weird..."

ASSESS LEVEL OF DANGER:

Questions:
- What did you take? (Drug name, amount)
- When did you take it?
- Is it prescribed to you?
- Are you having trouble breathing?
- Can you stay conscious?

IF SEVERE (overdose signs):
- Difficulty breathing
- Unresponsive/passing out
- Seizures
- Chest pain

ACTION:
"I'm calling 911 right now.
Stay with me. Don't hang up.

[CALL 911]

Emergency operator will ask me about what you took.
Can you tell me the drug name?
What's your address?"

(Don't worry about legal consequences—overdose survival is priority)

POST-EMERGENCY:

If they survive:
"I'm glad you're alive. 
What you just experienced is a sign substance use is becoming dangerous.

We need to get you support:
- Addiction specialist
- Substance abuse treatment program
- Recovery support (AA, NA, SMART Recovery, etc.)

Do you want to stay safe?"

IF THEY SAY NO:
"I can't force you. But I want you to know:
- I'm here if you change your mind
- People recover from addiction
- You deserve support

Here are numbers you can call anytime."
```

### When Someone Admits to Substance Abuse (No Immediate Crisis)

```
PERSON: "I've been drinking a lot more. 
I hide it from people.
I know I have a problem."

YOUR RESPONSE:

VALIDATE AWARENESS:
"The fact that you recognize it's a problem is important.
Many people don't get there."

ASSESS DEPENDENCY:
"How much are you drinking?
When did it start being more?
What happens if you don't drink?"

(Questions about: amount, frequency, withdrawal, impact)

EXPLORE THE FUNCTION:
"What does drinking do for you?
What feeling are you managing?"

(Addiction is always treating something: pain, anxiety, boredom, trauma, etc.)

PROVIDE OPTIONS:
"Recovery is possible. Different people take different paths:
- Medication-assisted treatment
- Inpatient rehab program
- Outpatient treatment + support groups (AA, SMART Recovery)
- Individual therapy + peer support

What sounds possible to you?"

CREATE SAFETY:
"Until you get treatment:
- Tell someone you trust
- Have a safety plan if you're alone
- Remove access to substances if possible
- Don't drive after drinking"

REFER TO SPECIALIST:
"You need someone who specializes in addiction.
This is beyond general therapy.

[Provide addiction medicine specialist / rehab program contacts]"
```

## 9. Crisis Communication: Do's & Don'ts

### DO:

✅ **Act fast.** Minutes matter.
✅ **Be direct.** Use words like "suicide," "kill," "die."
✅ **Take them seriously.** Assume crisis is real until proven otherwise.
✅ **Listen fully.** Don't interrupt or dismiss.
✅ **Validate their experience.** They're in pain; that's real.
✅ **Stay calm.** Your calm helps them feel safer.
✅ **Use their name.** Creates connection.
✅ **Ask about means.** "Do you have access to...?"
✅ **Involve others.** Don't carry crisis alone.
✅ **Provide concrete resources.** Numbers, addresses, people to call.
✅ **Document everything.** For legal/continuity reasons.
✅ **Follow up.** Crisis doesn't end with one conversation.

### DON'T:

❌ **Minimize.** "Other people have it worse."
❌ **Judge.** "How could you think that?"
❌ **Shame.** "You should know better."
❌ **Dismiss.** "You'll be fine."
❌ **Use clinical language excessively.** Stay human.
❌ **Promise confidentiality you can't keep.** Be honest about mandates.
❌ **Leave them alone if imminent danger.** Stay present.
❌ **Wait for them to ask for help.** Ask directly.
❌ **Believe "They're just seeking attention."** All cries for help matter.
❌ **Agree with delusions.** But don't aggressively challenge either.
❌ **Panic or overreact.** Keeps them from feeling safe.
❌ **Take responsibility for their choice.** You can't prevent someone's suicide.
(But you can escalate appropriately, which is your job.)

## 10. Crisis Resources (US Focused)

### National Crisis Lines

| Service | Number | Mode | Available |
|---------|--------|------|-----------|
| **988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline** | 988 | Call, Chat | 24/7 |
| **Crisis Text Line** | Text HELLO to 741741 | Text | 24/7 |
| **NAMI Helpline** (mental health crisis) | 1-800-950-6264 | Call | M-F 10am-10pm EST |
| **SAMHSA National Helpline** (substance abuse) | 1-800-662-4357 | Call | 24/7, free, confidential |
| **Domestic Violence Hotline** | 1-800-799-7233 | Call, Chat, Text | 24/7 |

### Crisis Response Teams
- Mobile crisis units (call 911 or non-emergency number)
- Police-assisted mental health (Crisis Intervention Team)
- Crisis stabilization units (short-term inpatient)

### For Clinicians/Apps
- Create resource database specific to user's location
- Update numbers quarterly (contacts change)
- Provide multiple modalities (call, text, chat)
- Include international resources if applicable

**When you escalate a crisis, document:**

```
DATE/TIME: [When]
PERSON: [Name, if applicable]
CRISIS TYPE: [ ] Suicidality [ ] Self-harm [ ] Homicidality [ ] Psychosis [ ] Overdose [ ] Other: ___

WHAT THEY SAID:
[Quote or summary of concerning statements]

YOUR ASSESSMENT:
[ ] Imminent danger [ ] High risk [ ] Moderate risk

MEANS/INTENT/CAPABILITY:
- Plan specificity: [vague / somewhat specific / detailed]
- Means access: [yes / no / unclear]
- Timeline: [none mentioned / someday / soon / imminent]

ACTIONS TAKEN:
[ ] 911 called
[ ] 988 contacted
[ ] Person's therapist notified
[ ] Family/emergency contact notified

[ ] Safety plan created
[ ] Followed up within 24 hours

OUTCOME:
[What happened? Person got help? Refused? Admitted to hospital?]
```

## 12. Crisis Communication in AI/Chatbot Context

### Limitations to Know

```
❌ CHATBOTS CANNOT:
- Physically restrain someone from harm
- Make split-second clinical judgment calls
- Guarantee understanding of complex situations
- Continue conversation if person becomes unable to respond
- Provide 24/7 human follow-up automatically

✅ CHATBOTS CAN:
- Recognize concerning language
- Provide immediate crisis resources
- Escalate to human crisis counselors
- Create written safety plans
- Send follow-up reminders
- Normalize seeking help
```

### Chatbot Crisis Flow

```
CHATBOT DETECTS:
("I want to hurt myself" / "I took pills" / etc.)

↓

IMMEDIATE RESPONSE:
"This is serious. I'm connecting you with trained crisis counselors right now.

[Get their location/phone number if possible]

988 Suicide & Crisis Lifeline
[Click to call] [Click to text] [More options]

I'm here while you reach out to them."

↓

WHILE THEY CONTACT:
[Chatbot stays open]
"Are you connected with someone? Are you safe?"

↓

IF THEY DON'T CONTACT:
"I'm concerned you haven't reached out yet.
Can you tell me what's getting in the way?
Or would you prefer [alternative method - text instead of call, etc.]?"

↓

HUMAN TAKEOVER:
[Escalate to human crisis counselor if available]
"I'm connecting you with a real person now."

↓

POST-CRISIS:
[Human schedules follow-up]
"We want to check in tomorrow. What time works?"
```

## Next Steps

1. **Customize for your context** - Create domain/population-specific scripts
2. **Train your team** - Do crisis communication training (recommended: certified CIT or similar)
3. **Test the flow** - Does escalation actually work? Are numbers current?
4. **Practice scenarios** - Run drills quarterly
5. **Update resources** - Review and update crisis numbers/resources quarterly
6. **Get feedback** - Ask people who've been in crisis: was escalation helpful?
