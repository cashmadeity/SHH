# Cultural Competence in Messaging

**Purpose:** Provide frameworks for creating communication that respects and integrates diverse cultural, spiritual, and identity-based mental health frameworks.

**Foundation:** APA Multicultural Guidelines, cultural humility principles, international mental health frameworks beyond Western psychology.

**Target audience:** Psychology app designers, therapists from majority culture working with diverse populations, international mental health teams.

---

## Core Principle: Culture Shapes What "Mental Health" Means

Western psychology defines mental health narrowly: individual wellbeing, symptom reduction, autonomy.

Other cultures define it differently:
- **Collectivist cultures:** Harmony with family/community is health; individual struggle is secondary
- **Spiritually-oriented:** Alignment with divine purpose or ancestral guidance
- **Holistic traditions:** Mind-body-spirit integration; physical/spiritual imbalance = mental distress
- **Communal healing:** Recovery happens in community, not individual therapy

**Our job:** Don't try to convert people to Western psychology. Meet them in their framework, then build from there.

## 1. Cultural Mental Health Frameworks (Beyond DSM-5)

### Framework 1: Collectivist/Family-Centered (East Asian, African, Latin American cultures)

**Core belief:** Person is interconnected with family/community. Individual suffering reflects family imbalance.

**How mental health shows:**
- Depression might appear as: family shame, concern about burdening others, loss of filial duty
- Anxiety might appear as: fear of disappointing family, worry about family's reputation
- Identity questions might appear as: conflict between individual desire and family expectation

**How to communicate:**

```
❌ INDIVIDUALIST APPROACH:
"You need to focus on your happiness.
If your family doesn't support that, maybe distance yourself."

✅ CULTURALLY-INFORMED APPROACH:
"You're balancing your own needs with responsibility to your family.
Both matter. What would honor both—your wellbeing and your family?
How can we approach this together?"
```

**Language adjustments:**

| Situation | Individualist | Culturally-Informed |
|-----------|--------------|-------------------|
| **Anxiety about family expectations** | "That's controlling. Set boundaries." | "Family expectations are important. What part feels reasonable to you? What feels like too much?" |
| **Depression triggered by family conflict** | "You should separate from them." | "Family relationships matter to you, which is good. How can you repair this? Who else in family can help?" |
| **Mental health shame** | "Your family is wrong to stigmatize it." | "In your culture, mental health issues carry shame. How do you want to handle this? Who might be more understanding in your family?" |

---

### Framework 2: Spiritually-Centered (Religious/faith-based cultures globally)

**Core belief:** Spiritual alignment or misalignment is foundational to mental health. Therapy without spiritual consideration is incomplete.

**How mental health shows:**
- Depression: Loss of faith, feeling abandoned by God/divine, spiritual emptiness
- Anxiety: Fear of judgment/punishment, living against spiritual values, need for forgiveness
- Trauma: Loss of sense of safety through faith, questioning divine protection

**How to communicate:**

```
❌ SECULAR APPROACH (ignoring spirituality):
"Let's use cognitive behavioral therapy to change your thinking patterns."
(No mention of their faith)

✅ SPIRITUALLY-INFORMED APPROACH:
"Your faith is central to who you are. Let's honor that.
What does your faith say about suffering? About healing?
How can we work with your spiritual beliefs, not against them?"
```

**Language adjustments:**

| Situation | Secular | Spiritually-Informed |
|-----------|---------|-------------------|
| **Grief with spiritual component** | "Accepting loss is the goal." | "Many faiths have wisdom about loss. What does your faith teach? How can we integrate that with moving forward?" |
| **Shame from religious teaching** | "Those beliefs are harmful." | "Religious teaching shaped you. Some parts help; some parts hurt. Which is which for you?" |
| **Feeling distant from faith** | "Religion isn't the problem." | "Your connection to faith used to sustain you. What shifted? Do you want to rebuild it?" |
| **Prayer as coping** | "Prayer alone isn't enough." | "Prayer is important to you. What other tools might help alongside your spiritual practice?" |

**Red flags (pathologizing spirituality):**
- ❌ Treating religious visions as hallucinations (without context)
- ❌ Saying "therapy will replace your need for faith"
- ❌ Dismissing prayer as avoidance (when it's genuinely sustaining)
- ❌ Not asking about spiritual beliefs before recommending treatment

---

### Framework 3: Mind-Body-Spirit Integration (Traditional Chinese Medicine, Ayurveda, Indigenous healing)

**Core belief:** Mental health is inseparable from physical, energetic, and spiritual states. Imbalance in one affects all.

**How mental health shows:**
- Depression: Stagnant energy (qi), weak life force, imbalanced elements
- Anxiety: Excessive heat, ungrounded energy, disrupted flow
- Trauma: Blocked channels, fragmented energy, disconnection from body

**Treatment approaches (in these frameworks):**
- Herbal medicine alongside therapy
- Energy work (acupuncture, Reiki, etc.)
- Body-centered practices (yoga, tai chi, dance)
- Seasonal/environmental alignment
- Dietary approaches to mental health

**How to communicate:**

```
❌ DISMISSIVE APPROACH:
"Those traditional practices don't have scientific evidence.
Let's stick with therapy."

✅ INTEGRATIVE APPROACH:
"Acupuncture and herbal medicine are part of your healing tradition.
I respect that. How is that supporting you?
What can I add that complements it?"
```

**Language adjustments:**

| Situation | Dismissive | Integrative |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| **Person using herbal medicine** | "Talk to your doctor; don't rely on herbs." | "What herbs are you using? How do they help? Let's make sure they don't interfere with any medications." |
| **Energy work/spiritual practices** | "That won't help your real problem." | "How do practices like yoga or meditation fit into your healing? What do they do for you?" |
| **Seasonal depression** | "SAD is a disorder we treat." | "Your sensitivity to seasons is real. Your tradition has practices for this. Let's combine them with what helps now." |
| **Body-based symptoms** | "It's psychosomatic; it's in your head." | "Your body is telling you something. What is it? How can we listen?" |

---

### Framework 4: Communal/Collective Healing (Indigenous, African Diaspora, LGBTQ+ found-family models)

**Core belief:** Healing happens in community. Individual therapy without community is incomplete. Historical trauma requires collective acknowledgment and healing.

**How mental health shows:**
- Individual depression reflects collective trauma (intergenerational, colonial, systemic)
- Healing requires recognizing shared struggle, not just individual symptom reduction
- Community rituals, witnesses, and collective action are medicine

**How to communicate:**

```
❌ INDIVIDUAL-FOCUSED:
"Let's work on your trauma in confidential sessions."

✅ COMMUNITY-AWARE:
"Your trauma is real and also connected to larger systemic things.
Who in your community helps you heal?
What rituals, gatherings, or collective actions support you?"
```

**Language adjustments:**

| Situation | Individual-Focused | Community-Aware |
|-----------|------------------|-----------------|
| **Intergenerational trauma** | "Your parents' experience isn't your responsibility." | "Your ancestors' trauma is in your body. Healing it honors them. How does your community process this?" |
| **Systemic racism/oppression trauma** | "Your anxiety is individual; let's manage it." | "Racism is traumatic. Your nervous system is responding to real threats. What community support helps you stay grounded?" |
| **Identity acceptance** | "Accept yourself; others' opinions don't matter." | "You need community that validates your identity. Who have you found? How can we build that?" |
| **Grief/loss** | "Process privately in therapy." | "In your tradition, how do we grieve together? What collective rituals help?" |

## 2. Identity-Specific Communication

### LGBTQ+ Identity

**Common experiences:**
- Coming out process (ongoing, contextual)
- Family rejection or conditional acceptance
- Healing from conversion attempts
- Dating/relationship safety
- Healthcare discrimination
- Allyship from allies vs. performative wokeness

**How to communicate:**

```
✅ DO:
- Ask pronouns and update them in chart
- Use their chosen name (not deadname)
- Ask about identity safety: "Who knows about your identity? Who's supportive?"
- Acknowledge healthcare discrimination is real (not in their head)
- Recognize LGBTQ+ community as source of identity, not dysfunction

❌ DON'T:
- Assume pronouns from appearance
- Question their identity ("Are you sure?")
- Frame LGBTQ+ identity as something to overcome
- Center straight/cis family's feelings
- Ignore violence/discrimination as factor in mental health
```

**Messaging examples:**

```
❌ NOT AFFIRMING:
"Let's work on acceptance of your family, even if they reject you."

✅ AFFIRMING:
"Your family's rejection is painful. And you also have/can build 
a chosen family and community that celebrates you.
Let's work on both: managing the grief of family rejection
AND strengthening chosen-family connections."
```

---

### Racial/Ethnic Identity

**Common experiences:**
- Racial discrimination and its mental health impact
- Code-switching (being different selves in different spaces)
- Pressure to represent entire race/ethnicity
- Internalized racism
- Healing from racial trauma/microaggressions

**How to communicate:**

```
✅ DO:
- Acknowledge racism is real (if you're not a therapist of color, especially)
- Ask about experience of discrimination (normalizes discussing it)
- Recognize strength/resilience required for survival
- Don't center white feelings ("I'm not racist, am I?")
- Build from cultural strengths, not just deficits

❌ DON'T:
- Blame individual coping for systemic problems
- Suggest therapy can fix discrimination
- Imply their concerns are "playing the race card"
- Ask "Where are you really from?" or other othering questions
- Pretend racism doesn't exist
```

**Messaging examples:**

```
❌ PATHOLOGIZING:
"Your hypervigilance about racism is a trauma symptom we need to reduce."

✅ VALIDATING:
"Constant vigilance about racism is exhausting, not a symptom.
It's a real adaptation to real threats. 
How can we help you have moments of safety and restoration?"
```

---

### Disability/Neurodivergence Identity

**Common experiences:**
- Grief about disability/neurodivergence + adaptation
- Discrimination and accessibility barriers
- Internalized ableism
- Identity-affirming vs. cure-focused approaches

**How to communicate:**

```
✅ DO:
- Ask about disability as identity + practical reality
- Distinguish between societal barriers and individual struggle
- Recognize disability/neurodivergence strengths
- Provide accessibility without being asked
- Say "disabled person" not "person with disability" (identity-first language, unless they prefer otherwise)

❌ DON'T:
- Center "overcoming" disability
- Suggest it's all in their head
- Provide unsolicited medical advice
- Assume what accessibility they need
- Use person-first language if they prefer identity-first
```

**Messaging examples:**

```
❌ CURE-FOCUSED:
"Let's work on overcoming your ADHD symptoms."

✅ IDENTITY-AFFIRMING:
"Your ADHD is real. Part of supporting you is 
helping you navigate a world not built for ADHD brains
AND honoring the strengths that come with your neurodivergence."
```

## 3. Cultural Humility in Action

**Cultural humility** ≠ cultural competence
- Competence implies you'll "master" culture (impossible)
- Humility means ongoing learning and self-reflection

### The 5 Practices of Cultural Humility

**1. Self-reflection**
```
Questions you ask yourself:
- What are my cultural assumptions about mental health?
- What culture shaped me? (Even if "colorblind," you have culture)
- Where am I likely to have blind spots?
- Do I center myself (white person, cisgender, able-bodied, etc.)?
```

**2. Mirroring/Checking**
```
Instead of assuming:
"In your culture, how is [mental health, family, spirituality] understood?"
"What's your experience? I don't want to assume."
"I might get this wrong—tell me if I do."
```

**3. Accountability**
```
When you mess up (and you will):
"I made an assumption/used wrong language. I'm sorry.
Help me understand better."

NOT: "I didn't mean it that way" or "I have friends from [culture]"
```

**4. Systemic awareness**
```
Recognize that mental health treatment happens in context of:
- Historical trauma (slavery, colonization, genocide, etc.)
- Current discrimination (racism, sexism, homophobia, ableism)
- Economic inequality
- Healthcare access disparities

Individual therapy can't fix these. But acknowledging them matters.
```

**5. Power sharing**
```
In design/research, include:
- People from the culture in decision-making (not just consultation)
- Community approval before launch
- Ongoing feedback from community members
- Revenue/credit sharing if applicable

Not: "We're bringing therapy to [underserved community]"
Yes: "We're partnering with [community] to create what they need"
```

## 4. Common Cultural Miscommunications & Repairs

### Miscommunication 1: Medication Resistance Misinterpreted as Non-Compliance

```
SCENARIO:
Person from culture where mental illness = family shame, medications = admitting weakness

THEM: "My family doesn't want me to take psychiatric medication.
They say it's shameful. I'm trying therapy instead."

❌ THERAPIST RESPONSE:
"Your family's beliefs are limiting your treatment.
You need medication. You have to stand up to them."
(Forces choice between family and treatment)

✅ REPAIR:
"I hear that medication carries shame in your family.
That's a real weight. Let's figure this out together.
- Is there a trusted family member who might understand mental health treatment?
- Would a doctor (vs. psychiatrist) feel less stigmatizing?
- Can we start with therapy while you explore medication separately?
- What would your family need to understand this isn't shameful?"
```

### Miscommunication 2: Prayer/Spirituality as Avoidance

```
SCENARIO:
Religious person using prayer as their primary coping

THEM: "Prayer helps me more than talking about problems.
That's how I cope."

❌ THERAPIST RESPONSE:
"Prayer is avoidance. You need to talk about your feelings."
(Pathologizes their coping)

✅ REPAIR:
"Prayer is powerful for you, and I respect that.
I'm wondering: after you pray, does the struggle get easier?
Or does it help differently?
How can prayer + talking help together?"
```

### Miscommunication 3: Family Involvement vs. Autonomy

```
SCENARIO:
Collectivist person who wants family involved; therapist trained in Western individual autonomy

THEM: "I want to include my family in therapy.
It affects them. Their input matters."

❌ THERAPIST RESPONSE:
"That compromises your confidentiality and independence.
Therapy is individual work."
(Western autonomy model as default)

✅ REPAIR:
"I honor that family is central to your healing.
We can include them in some sessions. Let's plan together:
- What parts are private (for you alone)?
- What parts do they need to be in?
- What goals do you all have together?"
```

## 5. Communication Across Language/Culture Barriers

### When Language Isn't Shared

**Challenge:** Words don't translate perfectly. Especially psychological terms.

```
PERSON (Spanish-speaking, using translator):
"Me siento triste y sin energia."
(Literal: "I feel sad and without energy")
(But "depression" might not be the word they use)

❌ ASSUMING TRANSLATION:
"You have depression. Here's an antidepressant."

✅ EXPLORING THROUGH LANGUAGE:
"Tell me more about that feeling without energy.
What does it feel like in your body?
Have you felt this before?
In Spanish, what do you call it?"
(Honoring their language as the more accurate one)
```

### Using Interpreters Ethically

```
✅ DO:
- Hire professional medical interpreters (not family members)
- Speak directly to person, not interpreter
- Brief interpreter about confidentiality before session
- Ask person if interpreter matters (some cultures it's reassuring; others uncomfortable)
- Use interpreters from same cultural/ethnic background when possible

❌ DON'T:
- Use family members (violates confidentiality, alters dynamics)
- Assume children can interpret medical information accurately
- Speak about person in third person ("Tell them...")
- Use untrained volunteers
```

## 6. Culturally-Responsive App Communication

### Translation ≠ Localization

```
❌ TRANSLATION:
English → Spanish, directly
(Words work; culture doesn't)

Example:
"Set boundaries with your family."
(Translates perfectly, but contradicts Spanish family values)

✅ LOCALIZATION:
English → Spanish, WITH CULTURAL ADAPTATION
(Words + culture)

Example:
"Respect your family while also taking care of yourself.
How can you honor both?"
(Maintains cultural value of family while still promoting wellbeing)
```

### Example: Multi-Cultural Messaging for Depression

```
ENGLISH/INDIVIDUALIST:
"Depression is your mind telling you something's wrong.
Let's identify and fix the problem."

SPANISH/FAMILY-CENTERED:
"When you're depressed, it affects your whole family.
How can we heal this together?
Your family's support matters."

CHINESE/TRADITIONAL MEDICINE:
"Depression is often imbalanced energy (qi).
Rest, gentle movement, and nourishing foods help.
Is a traditional healer part of your care?"

AFRICAN DIASPORA/SPIRITUAL:
"Your ancestors are resilient. Their strength is in you.
How is your community supporting you through this?
What rituals or gatherings help you connect?"

INDIGENOUS/LAND-BASED:
"Connection to land, community, and ancestors heals.
What practices ground you?
How can we support your cultural way of wellness?"
```

## 7. Cultural Competence Audit for Apps/Services

### Pre-Launch Checklist

- [ ] **Representation:** Team includes people from cultures you're serving (not just consultation)
- [ ] **Testing:** Beta tested with diverse cultural groups; feedback incorporated
- [ ] **Language:** Multi-language support (minimum 2; appropriate to population)
- [ ] **Content:** Examples/case studies reflect diverse families, beliefs, values
- [ ] **Accessibility:** Considers cultural communication styles (some prefer written, some oral, etc.)
- [ ] **Flexibility:** Allows users to honor family involvement, spiritual practices, etc.
- [ ] **Trauma-awareness:** Acknowledges historical/systemic trauma, not just individual
- [ ] **Values:** User can see their values reflected (not stereotyped)
- [ ] **Feedback:** Mechanism for users to flag cultural misalignment
- [ ] **Ongoing:** Plan for quarterly cultural review and updates

### Post-Launch Monitoring

- [ ] Track whether app works differently across demographic groups
- [ ] Collect feedback from diverse users (not just average engagement metrics)
- [ ] Review for cultural misrepresentation (who's using it? who's not?)
- [ ] Update messaging as you learn more
- [ ] Include diverse users in updates/new features

---

## 8. Red Flags: When NOT to Use This App

Your psychology app shouldn't be positioned as appropriate for everyone if:

- [ ] Only tested with one cultural/linguistic group
- [ ] Mental health model is explicitly Western only
- [ ] No mechanism for family involvement if user wants it
- [ ] Forces medical model when user prefers spiritual/traditional healing
- [ ] No translation/localization
- [ ] Examples are all from one ethnicity/culture/family structure
- [ ] Ignores systemic discrimination as factor in mental health
- [ ] Assumes users will abandon traditional practices

## 9. Integration with Ethics Framework

| APA Principle | Cultural Competence Application |
|---------------|---------------------------------|
| **Beneficence** | Benefit = respecting user's cultural framework, not imposing ours |
| **Non-maleficence** | Avoid: pathologizing culture, dismissing spirituality, forcing assimilation |
| **Fidelity** | Be honest about limitations (e.g., "Our team is mostly Western-trained") |
| **Integrity** | Represent diverse worldviews accurately, not stereotypically |
| **Justice** | Ensure equitable access and quality across cultural groups |
| **Respect** | Center user's cultural values as central to healing |

## Next Steps

1. **Inventory your assumptions** - What's YOUR culture's mental health model?
2. **Audit your content** - Is it culturally-centered or culturally-neutral?
3. **Partner with communities** - Hire/consult with people from cultures you serve
4. **Test with diverse groups** - Before and after launch
5. **Create feedback loops** - Ongoing learning from diverse users
6. **Train your team** - Cultural humility training for all staff
7. **Review regularly** - Quarterly check-ins on cultural relevance
