## Quick Access: Assessment Ethical Principles

### The 5-Point Assessment Ethics Framework

1. **Validity & Evidence** — Use tests with demonstrated reliability/validity for your purpose
2. **Competence & Boundaries** — Only assess when qualified; refer when needed
3. **Informed Consent** — User understands purpose, methods, risks, data use
4. **Cultural Adaptation** — Norms, interpretation adapted for cultural context
5. **Data Protection** — Assessment data treated as highly sensitive; secure throughout lifecycle

## Critical Principle: Assessment is an Act of Power

Diagnosis labels people. It can:

**Help:**
- Explain symptoms, reduce shame ("It's not a character flaw; it's anxiety")
- Open access to treatment & accommodations
- Guide evidence-based care
- Provide validation & understanding

**Harm:**
- Falsely label & stigmatize
- Trigger false treatment paths (wrong medication)
- Limit opportunities (insurance, employment, housing)
- Miss genuine needs (over-pathologizing one symptom)
- Ignore cultural context & meaning

**Therefore:**
Every assessment must ask:

**"How might this diagnosis help or harm this person?"**

If the harm outweighs the benefit → **don't deploy without additional safeguards.**

## Core Concepts: Before You Start

### Why Assessment Ethics Matters
- Assessment tools are **validated on specific populations** — may not work equally for others
- **Diagnostic labels have power** — can help or harm based on cultural context
- **Bias in assessment** comes from test design, norms, interpretation, not just AI
- **Competence requires training & supervision** — not something you can teach an AI alone
- **Informed consent is harder than you think** — users often don't understand what they're agreeing to

### What Makes Assessment Different from Therapy
| Aspect | Therapy | Assessment |
|--------|---------|------------|
| **Purpose** | Change/reduce symptoms | Measure/describe phenomena |
| **Power dynamic** | Collaborative | Evaluator has power (labeling) |
| **Informed consent** | Ongoing, adjustable | Point-in-time, may have lasting impact |
| **Bias consequences** | May limit effectiveness | May create false labels with legal/social impact |
| **Cultural adaptation** | High priority | CRITICAL (different norms/meanings) |

## Before You Launch: Critical Checklist

- [ ] **Validity:** Assessment has demonstrated reliability & validity for your purpose (Standard 9.01)
- [ ] **Competence:** Team includes licensed psychologists or qualified assessment specialists (Standard 9.02)
- [ ] **Consent:** Users understand purpose, methods, risks, data use in plain language (Standard 9.03)
- [ ] **Bias audit:** Test passed bias screening across demographic groups (see 02)
- [ ] **Cultural adaptation:** Norms, interpretation adapted for target populations (see 05)
- [ ] **Data security:** Assessment data encrypted, access controlled, retention policy clear (see 06)
- [ ] **Referral protocol:** Clear escalation path for concerning results (see 03)
- [ ] **Ethical audit:** Passed Level 1-2 pre-launch audit (see 07)

**Do NOT launch if any critical item fails.**

## Questions to Guide Your Work

1. **What am I assessing?** (trait, state, diagnosis, risk, ability)
2. **Who am I assessing?** (age, culture, language, ability status, context)
3. **Why?** (clinical decision, risk management, treatment planning, research)
4. **With what evidence?** (published tool, proprietary algorithm, AI model)
5. **How might this help?** (accurate diagnosis → appropriate treatment)
6. **How might this harm?** (false label → wrong treatment, discrimination)
7. **Am I qualified to interpret this?** (licensed? trained? supervised?)
8. **Can the user understand the results?** (plain language, cultural context)
9. **Is the data secure?** (who has access? how long kept?)
10. **What's my backup plan if results are concerning?** (referral path? crisis escalation?)
