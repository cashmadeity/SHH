# Informed Consent in Assessment

**Source:** APA Standard 9.03; HIPAA Privacy Rule; best practices in clinical assessment  
**Status:** Foundational for ethical assessment  
**Scope:** Designing consent processes for assessment that users actually understand

---

## Why Assessment Consent is Different from Therapy Consent

| Aspect | Therapy Consent | Assessment Consent |
|--------|-----------------|---|
| **Relationship** | Ongoing, collaborative | Single/limited, evaluative |
| **Power** | Client driven; therapist facilitates | Assessor/system has power (labeling) |
| **Consent timing** | Often renewed throughout | Point-in-time; then results are permanent |
| **User understanding** | Develops over sessions | Must be immediate & complete |
| **Consequence of misunderstanding** | Therapeutic delay | False diagnosis, wrong treatment, stigma |
| **Control** | Client can change direction | Results given whether user likes them or not |

**Key insight:** Assessment consent is harder because it's non-collaborative and the stakes (diagnosis, labels) are high.

## Elements of Real Informed Consent

### 1. Competence: Can the Person Actually Consent?

Before asking for consent, check:

```
COMPETENCE SCREENING

[ ] User is not intoxicated or under influence
[ ] User not in acute crisis (stabilize first, then consent)
[ ] User not experiencing severe dissociation/psychosis (capacity diminished)
[ ] User speaks/reads English fluently (or assessment available in their language)
[ ] User age appropriate (if under 18, parental consent needed)
[ ] User can read at 8th-grade level (understand consent document)

If ANY concern, hold off on assessment until person is more able to consent.
```

### 2. Disclosure: What Must You Tell Them?

**Non-negotiable disclosures:**

```
1. PURPOSE
   "We're assessing [what] to help with [why]"
   Examples:
   - "We're screening for depression to see if therapy might help"
   - "We're assessing ADHD symptoms to guide treatment planning"
   - "We're evaluating trauma to understand your needs"
   
   NOT: "We're collecting data" (too vague)

2. PROCEDURES
   "You'll [do what], taking about [time]"
   Examples:
   - "You'll answer 9 questions about mood (5 minutes)"
   - "You'll do timed cognitive tasks (2 hours)"
   - "You'll describe traumatic experiences (1 hour)"
   
   Specifics matter. Don't hide the time or difficulty.

3. WHAT IS/ISN'T THIS ASSESSMENT?
   "This is [what it is], NOT [what it's not]"
   Examples:
   - "This is screening for depression, NOT a diagnosis"
   - "This is cognitive testing, NOT a medical evaluation"
   - "This is a symptom check, NOT a treatment plan"
   
   Critical: Say what it's NOT, explicitly.

4. DATA USE & STORAGE
   "Your answers will be [used how, stored where, for how long]"
   Examples:
   - "Stored securely on our servers, accessed only by your therapist"
   - "Kept for 1 year, then deleted permanently"
   - "Used anonymously in research (only if you consent separately)"
   
   Be specific. "Secure" = encrypted on our servers, not just "kept safe."

5. WHO HAS ACCESS?
   "Your results will be seen by [who]"
   Examples:
   - "Only you and your therapist"
   - "Your doctor, with your permission"
   - "Shared with [insurance company] only if you request coverage"
   - "Not shared unless legally required (court order)"
   
   Be specific. Don't assume users understand "healthcare providers."

6. RISKS
   "Taking this assessment could [what risks]"
   Examples:
   - "You might feel sad reading results about depression"
   - "The assessment might suggest you have a condition you disagree with"
   - "Results could affect insurance, employment, custody (if relevant)"
   - "False diagnosis could lead to unnecessary treatment"
   - "False reassurance could delay needed care"
   
   Don't scare people, but be honest.

7. BENEFITS
   "Benefits might include [what]"
   Examples:
   - "Better understanding of your symptoms"
   - "Information to discuss with your doctor"
   - "Validation that your experience is real"
   - "Guidance on next steps"
   
   Don't oversell. Be realistic.

8. ALTERNATIVES
   "Instead of this assessment, you could [what]"
   Examples:
   - "Talk to your doctor instead (might be faster)"
   - "Try self-help resources (less professional input)"
   - "See a therapist directly (more comprehensive)"
   
   Show that assessment isn't the only path.

9. VOLUNTARINESS
   "You do NOT have to do this assessment"
   - "You can skip it anytime without penalty"
   - "You can decline without affecting your access to [other services]"
   - "Your decision won't be held against you"
   
   Make clear there's no coercion.

10. QUESTION & WITHDRAWAL
    "You can ask questions anytime"
    - "Contact us at [email/phone]"
    - "You can withdraw during assessment (results won't be scored)"
    - "You can request results deletion (limits apply, see policy)"
    
    Show how to get help, opt out, or change their mind.
```

### 3. Understanding: Did They Actually Understand?

After explaining consent, check for understanding:

```
COMPREHENSION CHECK (Ask, Don't Assume)

Pick 2-3 of these questions, ask user to explain:

1. "In your own words, what will you be answering questions about?"
   (User should describe topic, not read back script)

2. "What will happen with your results after you finish?"
   (User should know who sees them, how long kept)

3. "If your results suggest you might be depressed, what does that mean?"
   (User should know: NOT a diagnosis, might need to see doctor)

4. "If you decide you don't want to do this, what happens?"
   (User should know: No penalty, no problem)

5. "What could be a downside of taking this assessment?"
   (User should identify at least one risk you mentioned)

If user CANNOT answer these → Consent NOT sufficient
- Explain again in different words
- Use examples
- Check comprehension again
- If still unclear → Don't proceed; offer alternative
```

### 4. Voluntariness: Is the Person Pressured?

Look for subtle coercion:

```
RED FLAGS (Not truly voluntary):

[ ] "You have to do this to get [service]"
    (True necessary condition, or optional? Disclose clearly)

[ ] "Your doctor/employer/court ordered this"
    (Legal obligation exists; that's different from voluntary)
    
    How to handle: "You're required to do this by [reason]. 
    You can't decline, but you have the right to understand it."

[ ] "Everyone does this; you should too"
    (Peer pressure implicit)

[ ] "You already agreed (via Terms), so you're doing it"
    (Not truly voluntary if buried in terms)

[ ] App/therapist seems frustrated if user declines
    (Pressure, even if subtle)

[ ] Results are time-limited ("Do this before [date] or lose access")
    (Real urgency or artificial scarcity? Be honest)
```

### 5. Documentation: Did You Record Consent?

After consent, document:

```
CONSENT DOCUMENTATION

Date: [when consent obtained]
Method: [App click-through / Signed form / Verbal + notes]
Competence checked: [Yes / No / N/A]
Comprehension check: [Yes, user answered X correctly]
Key disclosure given: [checked boxes above]
User questions: [any questions they asked?]
User agreement: [Confirmed / Declined / Unclear]
Signature/Confirmation: [Button click / Signed / Verbal witness]

This documentation should be kept with assessment results
(for legal/ethical review if issues arise)
```

## Writing Consent in Plain Language

### The Problem: Legal Language

```
ORIGINAL (Legal, Incomprehensible):
"By accessing this assessment tool, you agree to terms of service 
that permit collection of personally identifiable health information 
which may be stored, processed, and disclosed per HIPAA regulations. 
User assumes liability for interpretation of results herein..."

PROBLEM: Most people don't understand this. 
Result: Not informed consent, just checkbox.
```

### The Solution: Plain Language (8th Grade Reading Level)

```
REWRITTEN (Plain Language):
"This app asks you questions about your mood to help screen for depression.

WHAT TO KNOW:
- This is NOT a diagnosis (only a doctor can diagnose)
- Your answers are private (stored securely)
- Results are kept for 1 year, then deleted
- You can skip this anytime
- If results worry you, we'll suggest talking to a doctor

Do you want to continue?"

Clarity check:
- Short sentences? ✓
- Simple words? ✓
- Specific, not vague? ✓
- Jargon explained? ✓
- User can act on it? ✓
```

### Readability Test

Before using consent language:

```
FLESCH READING EASE SCORE
(Automated tool: use FLE calculator online)

Score 90-100: Very easy (5th grade)
Score 80-89: Easy (6th grade)  ← TARGET
Score 60-79: Standard (8th grade)
Score 40-59: Difficult (college)
Score <40: Very difficult (grad school)

Write for 80-100 score (6th-grade level maximum)
```

## Consent Components (What to Include)

### Component 1: The Consent Screen (Before Assessment Starts)

```
[Simple header]
BEFORE YOU START

[Main disclosure in plain language]
This tool screens for depression. It takes 5 minutes.

[Specifics]
What we measure: How you've felt in the past 2 weeks
What we do: You answer 9 questions
Your data: Kept securely, only your therapist sees it
Duration: Stored 1 year, then automatically deleted

[What it's NOT]
⚠️ This is NOT a diagnosis. Only a doctor can diagnose.
⚠️ If results suggest depression, your next step is talking to a doctor.

[Risks & benefits]
Risks: You might feel sad reading results
Benefits: Understanding if you might benefit from help

[Choice]
Do you understand and want to continue?

[Buttons]
[I UNDERSTAND - START ASSESSMENT]
[SKIP FOR NOW]
[I HAVE QUESTIONS]
```

### Component 2: Mid-Assessment (If Getting Sensitive)

```
[If assessment asks about trauma, self-harm, abuse]

Just checking in: Some of the next questions might be upsetting.
You can:
- Take a break
- Skip questions you don't want to answer
- Stop anytime

Do you want to continue?
[YES] [TAKE A BREAK] [STOP]
```

### Component 3: Results Delivery

```
[Simple, clear results]

YOUR RESULT: Moderate Depression Symptoms

WHAT THIS MEANS:
Your answers suggest you might benefit from talking to a professional.

IS THIS A DIAGNOSIS?
No. Only a doctor can diagnose depression.

WHAT NOW?
Consider talking to:
- Your doctor
- A therapist
- Call 988 (24/7 crisis support, free)

[Resources with links]
- Find a therapist near you
- Learn more about depression
- Crisis resources

Questions?
Email support@[domain]
```

### Component 4: Privacy & Data Use

```
YOUR PRIVACY & DATA

What we collect: Your answers to assessment questions
How we use it: To show you results, improve this tool
Who can see it: Only you and your therapist (if applicable)
How long: We keep data for 1 year, then delete
Your rights:
  - See your data anytime
  - Delete your data anytime
  - Request a copy

Technical safeguards: Data encrypted, secure servers

[PRIVACY POLICY] [REQUEST MY DATA] [DELETE MY DATA]
```

### Component 5: Optional - Research Use

```
[SEPARATE CONSENT - only ask if you want to use data for research]

Can we use your answers in research?

We might use your answers (without your name) to improve the tool 
or publish research on mental health.

[ ] Yes, you can use my data anonymously in research
[ ] No, use my data only for my assessment results

(Only proceed if they consent OR if not required for main assessment)
```

## Special Cases: When Consent Gets Complicated

### Case 1: Parent Consenting for Minor

```
PARENT/GUARDIAN CONSENT (Under 18)

Parents should understand:
- What assessment measures
- Why it's recommended
- Risks & benefits
- How results will be used
- Whether child has any privacy (age-dependent)

Teen involvement:
- Age 13-17: Get BOTH parent consent AND teen assent
- "Assent" = teen understands and agrees (even if not legally binding)
- Teen privacy: Typically disclosed to parent, but discuss exceptions
  (e.g., in some states, teen can consent to mental health services without parent)

Documentation:
- Parent signature/confirmation
- Child's agreement documented
- Explain what teen will/won't see in results

Example language:
"[Parent name], this assessment screens for depression in teens.
[Teen name], you'll answer questions about your mood.
Your parent will see results. If we're concerned, we'll talk to your parent.
[Teen], do you feel okay about this?"
```

### Case 2: Mandated Assessment (Court Order, Employer)

```
MANDATED ASSESSMENT CONSENT

Situation: Assessment required, not optional

How to handle:
1. Disclose clearly: "This assessment is required by [who/why]"
2. Explain consequence: "Your results will be reported to [who]"
3. Explain limits: "You can't refuse, but you can understand it"
4. Explain rights: "You can see results, contest interpretation"
5. Ask consent anyway: "Do you understand and agree to proceed?"

Example language:
"Your employer requires this fitness-for-duty evaluation.
Your results will be reported to [HR/occupational health].
I'll explain results to you first, then submit.
Do you understand and want to proceed?"

Key: Even mandated assessments need informed consent to the process,
not just to the content.
```

### Case 3: Consent Via Proxy (Surrogate Decision-Maker)

```
SURROGATE CONSENT
(When person can't consent: severe dementia, incapacity, coma)

Get consent from:
- Legal guardian
- Healthcare proxy
- Next of kin (state-dependent)

What proxy needs to know:
- What assessment measures
- Why recommended
- Risks & benefits
- Person's prior wishes (advance directive, if available)
- How to withdraw consent if person seems distressed

Document:
- Who gave consent
- What you told them
- Person's current condition
- Plan to check if person wants to continue (if possible)
```

### Case 4: Assessment in Crisis

```
CRISIS CONSENT
(Imminent danger to self/others)

Rules:
- Crisis intervention can proceed without full consent
  (Life-safety overrides autonomy)
- BUT still explain as much as possible
- Consent is implicit: "I'll ask you questions to keep you safe"

Example: Person in suicidal crisis
"I need to understand what's happening so I can help keep you safe.
I'll ask some questions. Then we'll get you support.
Is that okay?"

After stabilization:
- Return to normal consent process
- Debrief about what happened
- Ask permission to follow up
- Document the emergency

Key: Crisis doesn't eliminate consent entirely; it modifies it for safety.
```

## Consent Failures: What NOT to Do

### ❌ Consent Fail #1: Vague Purpose

Wrong: "This app assesses your mental health"
Right: "This app screens specifically for depression using the PHQ-9 scale"

Problem: User doesn't know what they're getting into

### ❌ Consent Fail #2: Hidden in Terms

Wrong: Consent buried in 50 pages of Terms of Service
Right: Stand-alone consent before each assessment

Problem: User doesn't see it; clicks without reading

### ❌ Consent Fail #3: False Certainty

Wrong: "This assessment will determine if you have depression"
Right: "This assessment suggests whether depression symptoms might be present"

Problem: User thinks this is diagnosis; gets false confidence or false reassurance

### ❌ Consent Fail #4: No Opt-Out

Wrong: Assessment is mandatory to use app
Right: Assessment is optional; other features available without it

Problem: User can't make free choice

### ❌ Consent Fail #5: Consent Once, Use Forever

Wrong: User consents to assessment; now used for research, sold to companies, shared with insurers
Right: Separate consent for each use (assessment, research, sharing)

Problem: User didn't agree to what you're doing

### ❌ Consent Fail #6: Checking Comprehension, Not Really

Wrong: "Do you understand? Yes/No" (user clicks without thinking)
Right: "In your own words, what will you be answering about?" (user explains)

Problem: Fake comprehension check; no real understanding

## Consent Decision Tree

```
Is someone asking for assessment?

1. Are they ABLE to consent?
   YES → Continue
   NO (intoxicated, crisis, incapacity) → Special protocol (see above)

2. Have you DISCLOSED everything?
   [ ] Purpose
   [ ] Procedures
   [ ] What it is/isn't
   [ ] Data use & storage
   [ ] Who has access
   [ ] Risks & benefits
   [ ] Alternatives
   [ ] Voluntariness
   [ ] Questions & withdrawal
   
   If any unchecked → Add now, then continue

3. Is UNDERSTANDING CONFIRMED?
   Asked them to explain back? ✓
   Did they understand? ✓
   
   NO → Explain again, check again
   YES → Continue

4. Is VOLUNTARINESS CONFIRMED?
   No pressure? ✓
   Can decline without penalty? ✓
   
   NO → Remove coercion, check again
   YES → Continue

5. Is DOCUMENTATION DONE?
   Consent recorded? ✓
   Results will be with assessment? ✓
   
   NO → Document now
   YES → Green light for assessment
```

## Consent Checklist: Pre-Launch

```
CONSENT PROCESS
[ ] Separate consent before assessment (not in Terms)
[ ] Plain language (8th grade reading level)
[ ] Flesch score 80+ (easy to read)
[ ] All 10 disclosures included
[ ] Clear purpose statement
[ ] Explicit "what it's NOT"
[ ] Voluntary/no coercion
[ ] Comprehension check included
[ ] Documentation automatic (recorded with results)

CONSENT CONTENT
[ ] Assessment name/type clear
[ ] Time required stated
[ ] Risks explained (false positive, emotional impact)
[ ] Benefits explained (realistic, not oversold)
[ ] What happens to data (specific storage details)
[ ] Who sees results (therapist? employer? insurance?)
[ ] How long kept (1 year? deleted after?)
[ ] Can user refuse? How? (easy opt-out)

SPECIAL POPULATIONS
[ ] Minors: Parental consent + teen assent
[ ] Non-English speakers: Available in their language
[ ] Low literacy: Verbal option + written plain language
[ ] Mandated: Disclosed as such + explained rights
[ ] Crisis: Simplified + explained why urgent

ONGOING
[ ] Consent reviewed annually (is it still compliant?)
[ ] User feedback on understanding collected
[ ] Changes made if comprehension issues found
```
