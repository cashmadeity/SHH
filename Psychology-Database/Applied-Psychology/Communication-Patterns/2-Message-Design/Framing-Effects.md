# Framing Effects & Message Reframing

## Core Principle

The same objective information triggers different responses depending on **how it's presented** (the frame).

**Source**: Tversky & Kahneman (Prospect Theory); validated extensively in behavioral economics, health communication, marketing.

---

## Gain vs. Loss Framing

### The Classic Experiment: Asian Disease Problem

**Loss frame**: "Without this program, 400 people will die"
**Gain frame**: "This program will save 200 people"

**Same outcome, different psychology**:
- **Loss frame** → activates urgency, risk-taking, emotional response
- **Gain frame** → emphasizes positive outcome, rational consideration

**Neural difference**: Loss frames activate amygdala (emotion); gain frames activate prefrontal cortex (reasoning).

### Application by Domain

#### Games
| Frame | Application | Effect |
|-------|-------------|--------|
| **Gain** | "Earn 100 XP per quest" | Motivating, progressive |
| **Loss** | "Lose XP daily if inactive" | Urgent, engagement-focused |
| **Neutral** | "XP rate: 100/quest" | Objective, no emotional push |

**When to use**:
- **Gain frame**: Onboarding, low-motivation players, growth mindset
- **Loss frame**: At-risk retention, high-engagement players (already motivated)
- **Avoid loss frame for**: Anxious players, new players (increases pressure)

#### Marketing
| Frame | Copy | Psychology |
|-------|------|-----------|
| **Gain** | "Save $50 today" | Opportunity, positivity |
| **Loss** | "Miss out on $50 savings" | Urgency, FOMO |
| **Rational** | "Price: $199 (20% off list)" | Trust, transparency |

**Research finding**: Loss framing is 2x more emotionally activating but can trigger reactance if perceived as manipulative.

#### Therapy
| Frame | Application | When |
|------|--|---|
| **Loss** | "Without treatment, symptoms likely worsen" | Motivating commitment, preventing denial |
| **Gain** | "Treatment builds skills for long-term wellbeing" | Hope, meaning, self-efficacy |
| **Agency** | "You can develop strategies to manage this" | Autonomy, empowerment |

**Caution**: Loss frames in therapy can increase anxiety. Pair with agency/autonomy frames.

## Attribute Framing

**How many attributes you highlight and which ones**

### Example: Ice Cream Description
- **Quality frame**: "Made with real cream, fruit, vanilla bean"
- **Price frame**: "Only $4.99"
- **Health frame**: "Low-calorie option available"
- **Experience frame**: "Perfect for summer evenings with friends"

Each frame activates different decision criteria.

### Implementation
**Default order of attributes**:
1. **Most important to audience** (lead with their value)
2. **Differentiator** (what makes yours unique)
3. **Risk/concern mitigation** (address doubts)

**Example progression**:
- Analytical audience: Data → proof → price
- Emotional audience: Story → benefit → social proof

## Reference Point Framing

**What you compare something to changes perceived value**

### Examples

**Price perception**:
- "Only $9.99 per month (vs. $120/year for competitor)" → Seems expensive
- "$9.99/month (save $110/year vs. competitor)" → Seems cheap

**Health communication**:
- "5% will have side effect" → Seems risky
- "95% will not have side effect" → Seems safe
- (Identical information, different perception)

**Game progression**:
- "You're level 42/100" → 58 levels left (frustrating)
- "You've reached 42% completion!" → Progress toward goal (satisfying)

### Strategy
- **For motivation**: Reference upward (how far you've come)
- **For urgency**: Reference downward (how far to go)
- **For fairness**: Use multiple reference points

## Causality Framing

**Whose responsibility is implied**

### Example: Medical Communication
- **Personal agency**: "Your diet and exercise choices affect health outcomes"
- **Environmental**: "Community food deserts limit access to healthy options"
- **Provider**: "We'll help you develop a personalized plan"

Each frame implies different responsibility and agency.

### Therapy Application
| Frame | Message | Effect |
|-------|---------|--------|
| **Agency** | "You have the power to change patterns" | Empowering, activates effort |
| **Environment** | "Your circumstances influenced this pattern" | Reduces shame, validates experience |
| **Collaborative** | "Together we'll develop strategies" | Shared responsibility, therapeutic alliance |

**Caution**: Sole "personal responsibility" framing → shame; sole "not your fault" → helplessness. **Optimal**: "This happened to you (not your fault) + you can change it (your agency)."

## Concrete vs. Abstract Framing

**Specificity level**

### Examples

| Abstract | Concrete |
|----------|----------|
| "General health improvement" | "Blood pressure drops from 150 to 130" |
| "Better self-esteem" | "Confidence speaking up in meetings" |
| "Community engagement" | "Weekly guild meetups with 20+ friends" |

**Research finding**: Concrete frames activate behavior change; abstract appeals activate ideology/values.

### When to use each

**Concrete frames for**:
- Behavioral change (therapy, health)
- Onboarding (games: show specific mechanics)
- Conversion (marketing: specific benefits, not "better life")

**Abstract frames for**:
- Values-based decision (choosing brand/cause)
- Philosophy/meaning-making
- Inspiring action

**Optimal**: Lead with concrete (activates behavior), followed by abstract (justifies meaning).

Example: "Start with 10-minute walks (concrete) → Build long-term health (abstract)"

## Temporal Framing

**Time horizon and urgency implications**

### Examples

**Short-term frame**:
- "Today only: $50 off"
- "Immediate symptom relief"
- "Level up now"

**Long-term frame**:
- "Build lasting wealth"
- "Develop coping skills for life"
- "Invest in your future"

### Interaction with audience

| Audience | Short-term frame | Long-term frame |
|----------|-----------------|-----------------|
| **High time preference** (impulsive) | Effective, sometimes too effective | Ineffective |
| **Low time preference** (patient) | Feels gimmicky | More persuasive |
| **Mixed** | Start short-term, build to long-term | Blend both |

### Marketing timing
- **Awareness phase**: Short-term urgency ("This week...")
- **Consideration phase**: Medium-term value ("Over 3 months...")
- **Decision phase**: Long-term investment ("Years of value...")

---

## Social Framing

**In-group vs. out-group, norm vs. exception**

### Examples

**In-group**:
- "Join thousands of successful members"
- "Our community values..."
- "People like you already..."

**Out-group/exception**:
- "Different from the mainstream"
- "Only for the adventurous"
- "Against the grain"

**Norm vs. exception**:
- **Norm frame**: "Most people do X" → Conformity pressure
- **Exception frame**: "You're unique; customize X" → Individuality

### Application

**Games**: 
- Norm frame for new players (reduce uncertainty)
- Exception frame for veterans (honor customization)

**Marketing**:
- In-group frame builds community belonging
- Exception frame appeals to differentiation needs

**Therapy**:
- Norm frame: "This is a common struggle" (reduces shame)
- Agency frame: "Your path to recovery is unique" (respects individuality)

## Valence Framing

**Positive vs. negative emotion**

### Examples

| Positive frame | Negative frame |
|---|---|
| "Gain happiness" | "Reduce sadness" |
| "Build strength" | "Prevent weakness" |
| "Maximize pleasure" | "Minimize pain" |

**Research**: 
- **Approach motivation** (positive frames) → sustained, enjoyable engagement
- **Avoidance motivation** (negative frames) → short-term urgency, possible anxiety

### When to use each

**Positive frames for**:
- Sustainable behavior change
- Long-term engagement
- Building intrinsic motivation

**Negative frames for**:
- Immediate action
- High-stakes situations (medical emergency)
- Risk awareness (smoking cessation)

**Optimal combination**: "Prevent this problem + gain this benefit"

---

## Comparative Framing

**Relative to alternatives**

### Examples

**Absolute**:
- "This costs $100"

**Comparative advantage**:
- "This is $30 cheaper than Brand X"

**Comparative disadvantage acknowledgment**:
- "It costs more, but lasts 3x longer"

### Strategy
- **For price-conscious**: Lead with comparative advantage
- **For premium positioning**: Lead with quality, then explain value
- **For complex comparisons**: Multi-attribute matrix

**Marketing lesson**: Direct comparison can backfire ("Why are you attacking competitors?"). Use indirect comparison ("Industry leaders pay...")

---

## Default Framing

**What happens if you do nothing**

### Powerful effect: Status quo bias

People disproportionately choose the default option.

### Examples

| Domain | Default Impact |
|--------|-----------------|
| **Games** | Default difficulty, default class → most players use these |
| **Marketing** | Pre-checked options, default quantity → significant effect |
| **Healthcare** | Organ donation: opt-in (15%) vs. opt-out (85%) → massive difference |

### Strategic use (ethically)
- Set healthy defaults ("Auto-save progress")
- Make opt-out clear, not hidden
- Respect autonomy while influencing through structure

**Caution**: Invisible defaults are manipulative.

## Framing Menu for Communication Strategy

### For Games
1. **Onboarding**: Gain frame (positive) + agency (autonomy)
2. **Retention**: Mixed frame (progress + challenge)
3. **High-engagement players**: Loss frame (stakes matter)
4. **Struggling players**: Positive frame + support

### For Marketing
1. **Awareness**: Emotion + novelty + curiosity
2. **Consideration**: Concrete benefits + social proof + comparative
3. **Decision**: Urgency + scarcity + removing friction
4. **Retention**: Long-term value + community + meaning

### For Therapy
1. **Intake**: Validation (norm) + hope (positive frame) + agency
2. **Early treatment**: Concrete wins + visible progress
3. **Deep work**: Challenge + growth frame + collaboration
4. **Termination**: Legacy + skill transfer + meaning

## Framing Pitfalls

### Over-framing (too many frames)
**Risk**: Audience perceives manipulation
**Solution**: Lead with dominant frame; support with 1-2 others

### Inconsistent framing
**Risk**: "Why did they suddenly say the opposite?"
**Solution**: Maintain frame consistency; explicitly frame-shift if needed

### Matching frame to audience wrong
**Risk**: Message misses entirely (loss frame for loss-averse audience)
**Solution**: Test frames; adjust by audience segment

### Ignoring base rates
**Risk**: Framing makes low-probability event seem likely
**Solution**: Include actual probabilities with frames

## Research Validity Notes

**Framing effects are robust across**:
- 50+ years of research
- Multiple languages and cultures
- Health, marketing, political, and psychological domains
- Online and offline communication

**Known moderators**:
- Individual differences in decision-making style
- Cultural norms (some cultures more susceptible to certain frames)
- Emotional state (anxious people less sensitive to gains)
- Prior expertise (experts less susceptible to framing than novices)

**Ethical use**: Frames should illuminate truth, not obscure it. If you need to frame something, the truth should support all honest frames.
